**The Treasure of Uşas** (Japanese: ウシャス, also known as *Uşas* or *The Treasure of Usas*) is a 1987 action-platformer developed and published by Konami exclusively for the MSX2 home computer. It is one of Konami’s more obscure MSX2 titles but is highly regarded by enthusiasts as one of the best platformers on the system for its detailed graphics, inventive mechanics, excellent music, and atmospheric world-building.

It uses a **megaROM** cartridge (1 megabit) and features static-screen (flip-screen) gameplay due to MSX2 hardware limitations—no smooth scrolling. The game draws heavy visual and thematic inspiration from Hinduism, Buddhism, and Southeast Asian art and architecture (temples, statues, ruins). Music was composed by **Kinuyo Yamashita** (known for the original *Castlevania*). It lacks the SCC sound chip used in some other Konami MSX2 games but still delivers memorable PSG chiptunes, with moods subtly shifting the music pitch.

The game saw a Japanese release on November 1, 1987, and a European release the same year. It was later re-released digitally via Project EGG (2015) and on the Wii U Virtual Console in Japan (2016). A fan-made MSX2 prequel has been in development since around 2023.

### Story and Plot (Storyboard)
**Ancient Backstory**: Centuries ago, the war god Indra (or Induras/Indras) banished the supreme Mother Goddess Uşas (goddess of dawn) and shattered her powerful jewel into four pieces, scattering them to the winds.

**Modern Setup**: In the present day, Wit and Cles are lab assistants working for Assistant Professor Atre (or Atley) in the Oriental Archaeology department. The department faces financial ruin and potential closure. A newly discovered statue of the goddess Uşas has a recess in its forehead perfectly sized for a missing gemstone. An old map points to five ancient ruins along the Silk Road (inspired by real historical sites in South and Southeast Asia, such as areas linked to the Indus Valley Civilization, Gandhara, etc.) where the jewel fragments are believed to be hidden.

The pair sets out on an expedition to recover the four pieces (the game features five ruins/stages, with the fifth tying into the climax). Cute animated intermission scenes show Wit and Cles traveling—crossing rickety bridges, rafting down waterfalls, etc.—between ruins.

**Progression and Ending**: Players explore the ruins, defeat guardians, and collect jewel shards from animated statue bosses in temple rooms. Once the pieces are combined on the statue’s forehead back at the lab (or in the finale), a major twist occurs: the statue is revealed as a trigger/switch for an ancient nuclear weapon or doomsday device. The “treasure” is catastrophic. The Japanese version includes an extra downbeat epilogue screen (edited or removed in the European release) that mocks the heroes as “fools” with ironically cheerful music. The intro teases “If all four gemstones are found, something good is sure to happen!”—a darkly ironic setup.

The story blends adventure, archaeology, and mythological elements with a surprisingly mature, bittersweet twist for an 1980s platformer.

### Gameplay Mechanics
The core loop combines **traditional platforming**, **light RPG/stat progression**, **puzzle elements** (block pushing), and a unique **emotion/mood system**.

- **Dual Characters & Rescue Mechanic**: You control either **Wit Artie** (tall, uses a firearm/gun for ranged attacks; starts with lower speed and medium jump) or **Cles Stanley** (smaller, uses kicks for melee; starts faster but with lower jump). You choose one per sub-stage. If one character’s health depletes, they are “captured,” and the other can rescue them. Both dying ends the game. You can often return to the hub to switch characters (this resets puzzle blocks to their original positions).

- **Stats & Upgrades**: Each character has **VIT** (vitality/health resistance), **SPD** (walking speed), and **JMP** (jump height/power). Collect coins scattered throughout stages and press **F1** to access a shop menu to upgrade stats or heal health (10 coins per health bar segment—the only reliable healing method). Health bars are tracked separately per character.

- **Emotion (Mood) System — The Game’s Signature Feature**: Stages feature four collectible **kanji emotion icons**:
  - 喜 (**Joy** / Happy)
  - 怒 (**Anger**)
  - 哀 (**Sadness** / Sorrow)
  - 楽 (**Ease** / Good humor or neutral)

  Touching or collecting the icon changes the character’s current mood (displayed at the top of the screen). Moods alter:
  - Visual appearance and background music pitch (shifts by a semitone).
  - Attack properties (e.g., Wit’s gun can become a tri-shot, flame shot, or rapid-fire; Cles’s kicks can launch projectiles, become a roll attack, or gain rapid-fire on button mashing).
  - Mobility and puzzle interaction: In **Joy**, Cles can walk across gaps or step in mid-air, and Wit gains a mid-air/double jump; some moods disable or speed up block-pushing.
  - Boss access: Each stage’s boss door requires the **correct matching emotion** to open. Regular enemies often cause heavy knockback, which can send you back screens or dislodge your current mood—adding tension and backtracking.

- **Block-Pushing Puzzles**: Many areas require pushing or dropping blocks to create paths or platforms. Mood affects whether you can push them and how quickly.

- **Other Features**:
  - **Cartridge Combos**: Inserting certain other Konami games (e.g., *F1 Spirit*, *Metal Gear*, *The Maze of Galious*, *Nemesis*) into the second cartridge slot grants bonuses like extra coins.
  - Password system: Enter passwords at the title screen (press CTRL + password + ENTER) to jump to later ruins (e.g., “JUBA RUINS”, “HARAPPA RUINS”, “GANDHARA RUINS”, “MOHENJO DARO”).
  - Controls are somewhat clunky by modern standards but improve with stat upgrades. Spacebar (or equivalent) for attack/jump depending on context.

The game emphasizes careful mood management, exploration, and using both characters’ strengths.

### Levels, Structure, and Maps
The game consists of **five ruins** (stages), each functioning as a **hub world** with five doors:
- Four doors lead to multi-screen sub-stages focused on finding the correct emotion kanji, solving block puzzles, navigating traps/enemies, reaching and defeating a sub-boss demon (in the matching mood), and obtaining a key.
- After completing the four sub-stages, the fifth door (temple/shrine) unlocks. Inside is a single-screen room with a statue holding a jewel shard. Grabbing it animates the statue into a boss fight.

**Ruins are thematically inspired by real ancient sites** (passwords reference places like Harappa and Mohenjo-daro from the Indus Valley Civilization, Gandhara, etc.; other sources mention locations tied to Pegu/Bago, Dunhuang, Hunza, Alchi, Agra). Architecture features ornate pillars, statues, intricate patterns, and cultural motifs. Levels involve vertical and horizontal exploration across flip-screens, with tricky enemy placements and knockback designed to punish mistakes.

**Detailed stitched maps** of all ruins and sub-stages exist as fan rips (notably a large PNG by Fabio Albergaria hosted on vgmaps.com archives and previously referenced on msxsolutions.msxblue.com). These show the interconnected screen layouts, block positions, item/emotion placements, and boss rooms—highly recommended for completionists or TAS runners, as the game can be punishing with backtracking.

Inter-stage travel features short cute cutscenes. The final stages/build-up lead to the story’s twist confrontation.

### Characters
- **Wit Artie** (ウイット・アルティー): Born Nov 5, 1964; blood type B; 192 cm, 70 kg. Ranged gun user—generally more beginner-friendly.
- **Cles Stanley** (クレス・スタンレー): Born July 23, 1965; blood type A; 161 cm, 47 kg. Melee kicker—requires more precision but benefits from agility in certain moods.
- **Assistant Professor Atre/Atley**: The duo’s boss who sends them on the quest; prominent in the manual and intro.

### Enemies and Bosses
Regular enemies include temple guardians, mummies/zombies, and culturally themed creatures that often inflict heavy knockback. Sub-bosses are “demons” tied to each stage’s emotion. Temple statues animate into formidable bosses once the jewel shard is taken. The emotion system directly impacts boss fights (correct mood required to even access or effectively damage them in some interpretations).

### Reception and Legacy
Contemporary reviews (e.g., *The Games Machine*, *ASM*, MSX magazines) praised the graphics, level design, attention to detail, and “arcade-like” quality. It was called one of the best (or *the* best) platformers on MSX2 despite the lack of scrolling.

Retrospectively, it is celebrated for its ideas (emotion system, dual characters, hub structure, twist ending) but noted for clunky controls and high difficulty from knockback and backtracking. It remains a cult classic among MSX fans and collectors—complete copies command high prices today. A 2018 fan patch adds enhanced SCC-like sound. YouTube hosts full playthroughs and TAS runs.
