**Monkey Academy** (Japanese title: モン太君のいち・に・さんすう / *Montakin no Ichi-Ni-San Suu* or *Monta-kun no Ichi-Ni-Sansū*, roughly “Monta’s 1-2-3 Arithmetic”) is a 1984 edutainment/platform hybrid game developed and published by Konami for the MSX computer (also released on ColecoVision). It combines simple side-view platforming with basic arithmetic practice, aimed primarily at young children to make learning math fun through exploration and cute characters.

The game is often described as colorful and charming for its era, with flowing graphics and memorable animations (like happy monkey dances on success). It features flip-screen or fixed-screen platforming on small, explorable levels where players solve math problems by finding hidden numbers.

### Story / Storyboard
The premise is simple and whimsical, perfect for kids:

Monkeys are happily working on arithmetic problems, but one number is mysteriously missing (shown as a “?” in the equation). They suspect another monkey—or more likely the mischievous crab monster—has taken it. The player controls a brave monkey explorer who must search the level, locate the correct hidden number behind rolled-up window shades/blinds/scrolls on the walls, reveal and claim it, then deliver it to his helpful “girlfriend” monkey waiting above. 

By collecting three correct numbers (solving three problems), the stage is completed with bonus points and a celebratory animation of the monkeys dancing happily. The crab acts as the main antagonist/obstacle, trying to catch the player’s monkey. Fruits can be collected and thrown at the crab for points and defense. The overall tone is light-hearted, educational, and encouraging, with cute character animations reinforcing success.

There is no deep narrative or cutscenes—just this repeating cute setup across stages, with increasing math difficulty.

### Gameplay Mechanics
The core loop blends **platforming exploration** with **real-time math problem-solving**:

- **Math Problems**: An equation appears (e.g., 7 + ? = 12 or more complex variants) with one number missing. Players must calculate (or estimate at higher difficulties) the correct answer.
- **Searching for Answers**: Multiple rolled-up shades/blinds/scrolls are visible on walls at different heights across the level. The player jumps up to pull them down one by one to reveal hidden numbers. When the correct one is found, press the action button (SELECT or equivalent) to confirm—it rolls up and drops into the monkey’s hands as a “number bar” or answer item.
- **Delivery**: Carry the answer to a specific spot (marked by an arrow) beneath the helper “girlfriend” monkey at the top of the screen/area. Jump and pass it upward to her.
- **Enemy – The Crab**: A crab monster patrols a fixed, predictable path (often the same route every time, but it can feel sneaky or fast). Contact causes it to “bite” and eliminate the player’s monkey (lose a life). 
  - Defense/Scoring: Jump to grab scattered fruits, then jump again to throw them at the crab (500 points per hit). Fruits also give 100 points when collected.
- **Lives & Progression**: Start with 3 monkeys/lives. Earn an extra life every 20,000 points. Wrong answers show an “X” mark. Three wrong selections on the same problem cause a life loss (the crab then reveals the correct answer).
- **Time Pressure**: A timer runs; players must correctly solve and deliver **three problems** before time expires to complete the stage and earn a time bonus (10 points per remaining second).
- **Difficulty & Operations**: Before starting (or per stage progression), select the math operation:
  - Addition
  - Subtraction
  - Multiplication
  - Division (higher levels often involve estimation)
  - Possibly algebra-style problems in advanced modes.
  
  There are multiple difficulty tiers (around 4 levels mentioned in reviews). Stages progress or allow reselection with increasing complexity, more shades to check, trickier platforming, or faster crab behavior.
- **Controls** (Joystick or Keyboard):
  - Move left/right.
  - Jump (to reach shades, platforms, fruits, or escape crab).
  - Action/Confirm button to select revealed correct number.
  - Specific keys vary by system (e.g., spacebar for jump on keyboard; SELECT key to confirm).
- **Scoring**:
  - Correct shade found/selected: 500 points.
  - Successfully pass answer to girlfriend: 500 points.
  - Grab fruit: 100 points.
  - Hit crab with fruit: 500 points.
  - Time bonus for completing three problems quickly.
- **Multiplayer**: Supports 1–2 players (hot seat or simultaneous with multiple joysticks/keyboards in some setups). Demonstration/demo mode available if no selection is made.
- **Other**: Colorful tropical/jungle-like or indoor math-lab themed environments with platforms, walls full of shades, and the ever-present crab. Cute animations (monkeys dancing happily on success; sad or penalty animations on failure).

The game emphasizes speed, accuracy in mental math, spatial awareness (jumping/platforming), and quick decision-making while dodging the crab. It rewards practice and pattern recognition (crab’s fixed path is learnable).

### Levels, Structure, and Maps
The game features multiple stages/levels that increase in challenge. Each stage centers around solving **three math problems** sequentially within a time limit. 

- **Level Design**: Side-view platforming across one or more connected screens (flip-screen style common in 1984 MSX games). Layouts include platforms at varying heights, walls lined with rolled-up shades (multiple per problem, often 5–10+ to search), ladders or jumps between levels, and open space for the crab to patrol. Fruits are scattered for bonuses/attacks.
- **Visual Style**: Bright, cartoonish graphics with cute monkey characters. Levels have a playful, almost *Super Mario Bros.*-like feel in platform flow and collectible-hunting (shades instead of coins/blocks), though simpler and more static. Reviewers noted nice flowing graphics and cute monkey dance animations for the era.
- **Progression**: Solve three problems correctly → stage complete with bonus + happy dance animation → advance (or reselect difficulty/operation). Math complexity ramps up (basic addition early; multiplication/division/estimation later). Platforming may get slightly more vertical or require precise jumps as stages progress. Crab behavior or speed can intensify.
- **Maps**: No complex, large interconnected world maps exist (it’s not an open adventure). Each problem takes place in a compact, self-contained screen or small multi-screen area focused on exploration of shades and platform navigation. The crab follows a predictable patrol route, making levels somewhat puzzle-like once learned. There are no publicly detailed fan-made maps widely available (unlike pure action games), as the focus is on math + light platforming rather than maze-like exploration. Walkthrough videos (e.g., on YouTube) show full playthroughs, including higher/hardest levels (e.g., Level 5), revealing typical layouts with multiple shade rows, platforms, and crab paths.
- **Number of Stages**: Progressive through increasing difficulties; exact total varies by play (potentially many via difficulty loops), but core experience is short, replayable sessions focused on math practice.

The structure keeps sessions engaging and educational without overwhelming young players—short bursts of platforming + quick mental math.

### Characters and Elements
- **Player Monkey**: Cute explorer protagonist. Jumps, pulls shades, carries answers, throws fruit.
- **Girlfriend/Helper Monkey**: Waits at the top; receives correct answers and celebrates with dances.
- **Crab Monster**: Main enemy/antagonist. Patrols fixed paths; bites on contact; reveals answers after repeated mistakes.
- **Fruits**: Collectible bonuses and projectile weapons against the crab.
- **Shades/Blinds/Scrolls**: Interactive objects hiding numbers (0–9 or appropriate values). Jump to pull down.
- **Math Displays**: Equations with “?” prominently shown.

### Technical & Other Details
- **Platforms**: Primarily MSX (1984, Konami cartridge RC702 or similar); also ColecoVision (1984).
- **Media**: ROM cartridge.
- **Reception**: Positive for its era as fun edutainment—charming graphics, good music/SFX, effective math practice without feeling like boring drills. Some note it as surprisingly engaging platforming for a kids’ math game. Modern players appreciate its nostalgia and cute presentation. European manuals exist in multiple languages (English, French, German, etc.).
- **Manual**: Detailed instructions cover controls, difficulty selection, scoring, and tips (e.g., calculate first, then search quickly; learn crab patterns; use fruit defensively). Available via archives.
- **Legacy**: Obscure but fondly remembered Konami MSX title blending education with play. Emulators (WebMSX, etc.) allow easy play today. Walkthroughs and longplays exist on YouTube showing full runs, including hardest difficulties.

In short, *Monkey Academy* is a delightful, light-hearted 1984 Konami MSX gem that successfully merges accessible platforming with practical arithmetic learning. Its cute characters, predictable-but-challenging crab, shade-hunting mechanic, and progressive math difficulties make it replayable and educational. It lacks the depth or complexity of pure action titles like *Uşas* but excels in its niche as charming edutainment.
