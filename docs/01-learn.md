**The latest research provides a strong, evidence-based foundation** for designing a game that helps 9–12-year-olds (especially those struggling with calculations like multiplication, fractions, and division) build deep understanding rather than just rote procedures. Your goal of *gamifying the lessons* aligns well with what works: high engagement + visual models + conceptual insight + targeted practice.

### Key Research Insights (2020–2026 and Foundational Evidence-Based Guides)

**1. Conceptual Understanding Must Come First (or Intertwine with) Procedural Fluency**  
The gold-standard **IES What Works Clearinghouse Practice Guide** (*Developing Effective Fractions Instruction for Kindergarten Through 8th Grade*, 2010 — still the most cited rigorous synthesis) gives five recommendations with moderate evidence for several. It stresses building on children’s informal knowledge of *sharing* and *proportionality*, using visuals to show *why* procedures work, and addressing misconceptions (e.g., “add the tops and bottoms”).

The **NCTM Principles to Actions** (2014) outlines 8 Effective Mathematics Teaching Practices that remain the benchmark:
- Establish clear goals within learning progressions.
- Use and connect multiple representations (visuals, symbols, contexts).
- Facilitate discourse and pose purposeful questions.
- Build procedural fluency *from* conceptual understanding.
- Support productive struggle.
- Elicit and use student thinking.

A major **2025 Psychological Science in the Public Interest report** (“What the Science of Learning Teaches Us About Arithmetic Fluency”) synthesizes developmental cognitive science: Fluency is *not* just fast rote recall — it includes noticing number relationships and using strategies flexibly. The most effective cycle is:
1. Ground in conceptual understanding + explicit strategies.
2. Brief, well-timed retrieval practice (once accurate).
3. Return to discussion/reflection.

This cycle works for multiplication facts, multi-digit work, and fractions.

**2. Leverage How Children’s Brains Naturally Process Ratios and Magnitudes**  
A 2025 brain-imaging study (Waisman Center, UW-Madison) found that children as young as 7 already use specialized right-parietal-frontal networks to process *non-symbolic ratios* (e.g., comparing clusters of dots or line lengths) as holistic magnitudes. School instruction “recycles” these same circuits for symbolic fractions (like 2/3). Traditional step-by-step teaching often fights this intuition, increasing anxiety and errors. Implication: Start with or strongly connect to magnitude comparison, relative size, and visual ratios — it feels more natural and reduces “whole-number bias” mistakes.

**3. Game-Based Learning (GBL) Has Positive but Moderate Effects — Design Quality Matters**  
Multiple meta-analyses (including 2022–2025 reviews) show small-to-moderate positive effects on math achievement (effect sizes roughly 0.13–0.41). Games work especially well in primary/intermediate grades when they:
- Provide immediate feedback and adaptivity.
- Embed concepts in meaningful contexts or narratives.
- Use visuals and interactivity (not just drill).
- Support engagement without replacing conceptual depth.

Pure “gamified worksheets” or endless drills show weaker results; games aligned with the principles above (visual models, reasoning, reflection) perform better.

**Common Misconceptions to Target in the Game** (from IES and classroom research):
- Treating numerators/denominators as separate whole numbers.
- Believing “bigger denominator = smaller fraction” without magnitude sense.
- Forgetting to redefine the unit in fraction multiplication (“half of a half”).
- Confusing division procedures (invert-and-multiply without understanding why).

### Visual Models That Research Strongly Supports (Use These Heavily in Your Game)

**Number Lines** (moderate evidence in IES guide; excellent for magnitude, equivalence, ordering, operations, and connecting to whole numbers/decimals).  




**Area Models** (especially powerful for fraction multiplication/division — shows *why* the algorithm works by redefining the unit).  




**Arrays** (for multiplication facts and the multiplication–division relationship/inverse operations).  




These models are perfect for interactive game mechanics (drag, snap, shade, animate, compare).

### Practical Recommendations for Designing Your Game

**Core Pedagogical Loop (Research-Aligned)**:
1. **Explore/Introduce** — Narrative context + interactive visual model (number line, area, array). Let kids discover relationships.
2. **Apply/Reason** — Puzzle or story problem in a real-world or fantasy context (sharing treasure fairly, scaling a map, baking with fractions, racing with fractional distances/speeds).
3. **Practice Fluency** — Short, adaptive retrieval or procedural challenges with immediate visual feedback + explanatory hints.
4. **Reflect/Connect** — Quick “why did that work?” moment or comparison (e.g., “How is this like the array you used earlier?”). This closes the 2025 science-of-learning cycle.

**Topic-Specific Ideas**:
- **Multiplication (facts → multi-digit)**: Start with arrays/area models for conceptual understanding and properties (distributive, commutative). Progress to partial-products or standard algorithm *after* visuals. Link directly to division as inverse. Games: “Build the strongest army by multiplying groups” or area-filling challenges.
- **Fractions**: Heavy emphasis on magnitude (number-line positioning/comparing battles), equivalence (matching or transforming visuals), and operations with models *before* symbolic rules. Use sharing stories early, then move to “fraction of a fraction.” Real contexts: recipes, dividing land/pizza fairly with twists, scaling quantities.
- **Division**: Conceptual first (fair sharing/grouping with visuals and remainders), then connect explicitly to multiplication inverse. Visual long-division or ribbon/number-line models for fraction division. Story: “Distribute resources fairly in the kingdom while monsters appear if you’re inefficient.”

**Gamification & Engagement Features That Research Supports**:
- Strong narrative/adventure (helps with real-world contexts and motivation).
- Progressive levels that scaffold from concrete visuals → semi-concrete → abstract/symbolic.
- Adaptive difficulty + smart hints (e.g., “Would a number line help here?” or auto-show model).
- Immediate visual + explanatory feedback (models light up or animate correctness).
- Low-stakes practice zones + “conceptual bonus” challenges (explain or compare strategies).
- Spaced/interleaved practice (mix multiplication, division, and fractions across sessions).
- Optional collaboration or light competition (for discourse/motivation — watch for anxiety).
- Progress tracking that celebrates *understanding* (e.g., “You mastered magnitude sense!”) not just speed/accuracy.
- Customization (avatars, themes) to increase ownership for 9–12-year-olds.

**For Children Who Struggle (like your 11-year-old)**:
- Begin with their informal strengths (sharing stories, comparing sizes).
- Over-use visuals and manipulatives (digital versions are ideal — draggable, resizable, color-coded).
- Build number sense/magnitude first (many calculation errors stem from weak magnitude understanding).
- Short sessions, frequent success experiences, and explicit strategy instruction.
- Productive struggle with scaffolding (not frustration).

### Next Steps to Build the Game

This research gives you a solid blueprint: **prioritize interactive visual models (number lines, area models, arrays), real or story contexts, conceptual insight before pure procedures, and the conceptual → practice → reflect cycle**.
